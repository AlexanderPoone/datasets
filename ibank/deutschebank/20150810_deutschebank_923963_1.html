Industry Update



Preliminary China trade data

Grant Sporre

Research Analyst (+44)20 754-58170 grant.sporre@db. com


Limited evidence of improving demand

Signs of a second half recovery in China's material sector has been elusive so far. The preliminary trade data gives the market little to suggest any meaningful improvement, nor does the data suggest a significant deterioration. The decline in aluminium imports is indicative of a supply side response to weak prices in our view. The strong rebound in iron ore imports without an increase in port stocks not only indicates a supply recovery from the seaborne market, but potentially some restocking at the mill level in anticipation of an improvement in domestic demand. However, the increase in steel exports would suggest that this improvement is not imminent.

A glimmer of hope for aluminium, but not for steel

Exports of both steel and aluminium are up 28% year to date, reinforcing the view that in both industries, Chinese overcapacity is being exported. The debate as to whether this is a temporary relief valve, or a fundamental shift in the industry remain. Our view is that the shift in aluminium is more structural with the combination of cheaper domestic coal (by virtue of industry restructuring and consolidation) and newer technology affording China a cost advantage. This month's data however indicates a temporary respite in the trend of increasing exports, with aluminium semi-exports down 20% month on month and down 5% year on year. The combination of weaker US premiums, an intensified focus on the export of "fake" semi's, in particular China Zongwang, and some high cost Chinese closures has likely contributed to this trend. Whilst this may indicate limited downside in price, there is an overhang of redundant capacity.

A strong rebound in iron ore imports

Chinese iron ore imports are now flat year to date with a strong rebound in July, up 4% year on year and 15% month on month. Given the small drop in volumes from the BHPB/FMG Port Hedland port, this would suggest a recovery from both Brazil and Rio’s two ports. This would be in line with channel checks on shipping rates. The combination of lower production and stable iron ore port inventories would suggest some restocking at the mil levels. We continue to think that further closures in some of the Australian and Brazilian mid-tier miners is required to ensure some degree of market balance and price support.

Copper imports remain steady

The demand for copper cathodes has been weak so far, with imports down 9% year to date. However, this "shortfall" has been compensated by imports of concentrate and domestic cathode production. The July data point which is essentially flat month on month gives nothing to either the bulls or the bears in our view. We estimate Chinese apparent copper demand is up 2.2% for H 1'15.

We value the miners using a discounted cash flow analysis at the asset level. Downside risks include a hard landing in China, where the easing monetary conditions fail to translate into stabilising metal demand. Upside risks include a significant broad ranging Chinese fiscal stimulus.
