Better server outlook and manageable smartphone risks

Factoring in the impact of two acquisitions

Following a more detailed review of Lenovo's IBM server and Motorola acquisitions (assuming completion in 3QFY15), we factor our assumptions of their impact into our financial model (Figure 1). We raise our FY15E and FY16E sales by 10% and 21% and cut EPS estimates by 13% and 11% to US$8.37 and 9.71 cents to reflect the possible P&L impact of these acquired businesses and their share dilution (7.75%). We hold a more positive view on Lenovo to turn around these two businesses and believe investors may underestimate its execution and IBM/Google's support to help the integration. We reiterate our Buy rating with a new target price of HK$10.4 (from HK$12).

Clear strategies, solid execution to turn Motorola around

We are positive Lenovo can easily lift Motorola's gross margin to >15-20% by improving its product portfolio (only two refresh models in 2013), shipment scale, cost structure and brand presence (the dual brands) in Asia and Europe. Given Motorola's better brand position, higher ASP (>US$250 vs. Lenovo's <US$90), and Lenovo's excellent execution and supply chain management, the integration of its newly acquired smartphone operation will be easier than its purchase of IBM's PC business, which took six quarters to reach turnaround.

We are optimistic on IBM server acquisition

We expect Lenovo to make money immediately from its newly-acquired server business in 3QFY15. Our higher confidence (FY15E and FY16E OPM at 3.5% and 4.6%, Figure 1) vs consensus (slightly profitable) not only comes from IBM's better product portfolio with higher gross margins (25%+ vs. Lenovo's 15-16%) but Lenovo's leverage of its commercial PC strength and home market advantage in China to boost its sales and profitability. Its rising server profit could help ease smartphone losses.

Valuation and risks

We base our target price on 16x FY15E EPS. We award a higher P/E multiple to Lenovo than to its peers (9-14x) because of its robust earnings growth and market share gains from its PC business, potential upside from its server operation and strong smartphone and tablet shipments. Downside risks: weaker demand for PC, smartphone, server and tablets, and channel inventory.
