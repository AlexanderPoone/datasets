Please sir, I want some more

The toll of the downshift in growth momentum since midyear is set to be revealed in upcoming 3Q21 GDP reports. China kicked off with a material disappointment—our seasonal adjustment points to a sequential contraction— that should be followed by a slowdown to a 3%ar in the US owing to a surprising outright contraction in domestic final sales. We anticipate our forecast for a strong 8.5%ar flash GDP growth reading from the Euro area next week will buck this trend and confirm that global GDP growth expanded at an above-potential 3.4%arpace last quarter. However, this outcome would represent a substantial step back in the speed of the recovery. Relative to our forecast at the start of last quarter, the 3Q21 global GDP shortfall from its prepandemic pace has deepened by over a percentage point and now points to a 2.7% journey back to a complete recoveiy (Figure 1).

Some drivers of momentum loss should soon fade as Delta drag and supply bottlenecks abate and China’s policy turns more supportive. In addition, the Euro area bounce points to a large boost still to come in countries (concentrated in the Asia/Pacific region) where vaccination rates and mobility are now moving substantially higher. We thus forecast global GDP growth to rebound to a 4.5%ar average this quarter and next. We must temper this optimism, however, by recognizing that the projected rebound does not make up for last quarter’s disappointment. While vaccinations continue to rise, the travel shortfall during last quarter’s holiday season will have lingering effects as will the elevated goods prices that squeezed household purchasing power. At the same time, energy price pressures continue to build and the rise in inflation is producing a shift toward faster monetary policy normalization.

The silver lining of a less boomy growth profile is that it could prove conducive to a more sustainable recovery. The stunning bounce back from last year’s pandemic collapse revealed the limits to ramping up supply in response to surging demand—as manifested in manufacturing bottlenecks and multidecade highs in core goods inflation (Figure 2).
