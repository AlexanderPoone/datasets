Even though the presidential election is still more than six months away and it is too early to draw strong conclusions from polls, it seems that former President Lula’s lead over President Bolsonaro has been diminishing of late. In that sense, the highlight this week was a Futura/Modalmais poll that showed the two candidates technically tied and still far ahead of the other candidates (Table 1). In other polls, however, the margin in favor of Lula is larger, but it is narrowing, as Bolsonaro’s rejection rate seems to be decreasing.

As we highlighted about six months ago, this past week was a key marker on the political calendar, as those who intend to be a candidate in this year’s elections had to leave top public positions such as ministries by April 1 and be affiliated with a party. Former Justice Minister Sergio Moro, running third in most polls, changed parties and abandoned the presidential race for now to avoid the polarization evident in the polls (“Em nota, Moro confirm a ida para Uniao Brasil e desiste de pre-candidatura,” Broadcast, March 31, 2022). In turn, Joao Doria left his post as governor of Sao Paulo state and announced his pre-candidacy for president. A cabinet reshuffle also occurred as many ministers intend to run for different executive and legislative positions.

It is still unclear whether Sergio Moro’s decision, amid the confirmation of Joao Doria’s pre-candidacy, could boost President Bolsonaro’s support or open the way for a consolidation of votes around a third-party candidate that is neither Lula nor Bolsonaro. In any case, we expect the election noise to pick up from now, particularly in the second half of the year.

Meanwhile, pressures for a wage increase for civil sen-ants returned, with the BCB, IRS staff, and federal auditors going on strike (“Servidores do Banco Central aprovam greve a par-tir de 1/4,” Broadcast, March 28, 2022) and rumors that the government is mulling a 5% increase for all federal public servants (“Governo avalia reajuste salarial linear de 5% para servidores partir de julho,” (9 Globo, March 31, 2022) before the deadline set by the electoral law (90 days before the election).

Unemployment rate dropped further in February

In February, the unemployment rate remained at 11.2%, lower than our be low-consensus call of 11.3%. Seasonally adjusted, the jobless rate decreased from 11.6% in January to 11.3%, the lowest rate since mid-2016. Our monthly proxy is even lower than that but was unchanged in February (Figure 1). Employment, which decreased in January, increased 0. l%m/m sa in February and reversed most of its January weakness, which likely was due to the spike in COVID-19 cases early in the year. This reversal from January to February occurred particularly in informal jobs. Another decline in the participation rate, which remains below pre-pandemic norms, also contributed to the falling unemployment rate.

The quits rate, a firm-side measure of slack in the labor market, has increased recently and is suggesting another leg tighter in conditions. Our firm-side measure of the unemployment rate has been stable around 8%, still well below the official rate, which remains above 11%.

After growing 0.8%m/m sa in January, real wages edged up by 0.1% in February, the second monthly rise after a long stretch of declines. As the composition effect of faster growth in low-wage employment fades, average nominal wage growth accelerated to 0.6%oya, but real wages fell 8.8%oya given the high rate of inflation. Looking at sector-level data to avoid this composition effect, we see that nominal wages are accelerating across the board and are rising in many sectors of the economy.
