I’m beginning to believe you

Market moves this week seemed to have capitulated to the Fed view that rates will be higher for longer and that a pivot might not necessarily come as soon as some might expect. Front-end Treasury yields, the most sensitive sector to near-term monetary policy expectations, rose this week. In particular, 2y Treasury yields increased 2 Ibp week over week to 4.51%. OIS forwards also moved farther into restrictive territory, with the peak funds rate now priced at nearly 5.19% versus 5.03% on Friday 2/3 and 4.89% on Thursday 2/2 (pre jobs report), and the OIS curve in 2H23 steepened by ~15bp since Thursday 2/2 (Exhibit 1). This is now more in line withour economists’ expectations of 25bp rate hikes in March and May and a terminal range of 5.00-5.25%, though we continue to diverge slightly from markets in terms of Fed expectations in late 2023.

To be sure, this week’s Fedspeak was decidedly hawkish (Exhibit 2). Chair Powell spoke on Tuesday, reiterating his talking points from the recent Fed meeting: disinflation has begun but still has a long way to go, and further interest rate increases are likely needed (see Go your own hw, M. Feroli, 2/7/23). Warning that a continuing strong labor market could mean a higher funds rate peak, Powell once again stressed policy’s dependence on data. Other Fed officials echoed a similar-sentiment, generally asserting that the Fed has more work to do to curb inflation and that rates will likely need to remain higher for longer. To that end, initial claims for the week ending February 4 increased 13K to 196K—the first weekly increase since mid-December, but still very low and indicative of continued strength in the labor-market—continuing to backup hawkish Fed sentiment (see US: Initial claims mow up but stay very low, D. Silver, 2/9/23). Next week’s CPI print for January will further shed light on whether there is more work for the Fed to do.
