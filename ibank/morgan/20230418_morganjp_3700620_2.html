Japan equity volatility: an asymmetrical opportunity for long investors

The recent decline in Japan equity volatility presents an opportunity for long volatility investors (Table 1). Our analysis suggests that there is an asymmetrical risk in favour of long volatility investors at this juncture. In this report, we will provide an overview of the factors driving Japan equity volatility and recommend building long exposure to Japan equity volatility.

Volatility floor and dealer’s derivatives hedging flows

According to the post-pandemic shock history, we observe that the Nikkei’s realized volatility has a minimum level of ~15v when the Nikkei spot trades above 29000 (Figure 1). This can be attributed in part to the concentration of structured product knock out barriers around the 30,000 level in recent years, which has led to an increase in speculative call option buying flows aimed at positioning for a potential Nikkei breakout.

While dealers’ derivatives hedging flows have been a major volatility dampening factor, our gamma positioning proxy suggests dealer’s long gamma exposure has declined to less influential levels (Figure 2). The gamma positioning proxy considers delta hedging flows that include listed options, autocallables and leveraged ETFs. The total gamma is the dollar value of the index that needs to be traded (to adjust all delta hedges) following a 1% move in the market. The delta-hedging flows of long gamma positions can be described as buy low and sell high and has an impact of decreasing realized volatility. At a spot level of 28,000 in Nikkei, we estimate dealers are long US$0.8bn of gamma, which is equivalent to ~5% of 3M average turnover in futures. The current profile implies that dealers are no longer providing significant volatility dampening flows, which is a positive development for long volatility investors.
