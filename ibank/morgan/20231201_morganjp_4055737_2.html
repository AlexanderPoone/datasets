Hints of improvement in industry

For over a year, global manufacturing has effectively stalled alongside only a modest gain in final demand and a drawingdown in the pace of inventory accumulation. Output looked to be perking up around midyear but this recovery was narrowly

based in the Asian tech sector. Gains have been softer and similarly mixed in September and October, and global manufacturing growth looks to have once again slowed to a stall.

While the global PMI has not tracked the pace of growth in factory output well in recent years, it has provided a reasonable guide to momentum swings. This is important given that growth in global factory output has been both noisy and uneven. The global output PMI for November came in at 49.9 (Figure 3). While this is still only consistent with 0.3%ar growth in global manufacturing, the Ipt rise more than reverses the October drop, tempering the concern that activity was sliding into contraction. Importantly, the November gain in Europe, a region where the outlook has been particularly weak—and where the surveys tend to be the most accurate— is encouraging. As a bellwether for global industry, Asia also saw a notable rise in output PMIs, driven by Mainland China, Korea, and Taiwan.

The improvement in Asia is echoed in the trade data but still appears somewhat tentative. Exports out of EM Asia rose 6.3%3m/3m, saar in October, while the tone from the for-ward-looking new export orders PMI dipped in November and suggests some moderation. More generally, the manufacturing recovery in Asia remains narrowly driven by tech, led by Korea and Taiwan but with Japan recently joining. Still-sluggish non-techproduction, combined with limited capex growth across the region, points to cautious corporate sentiment. For the tide to turn backup, better news on final demand growth will be needed. In this regard, the sizable bounce in the global investment goods PMI is encouraging, as it suggests a modest pickup in global capex growth after stalling for much of 2H23.
