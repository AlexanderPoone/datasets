Hong Kong: March CPI rises 3.0%oya, holiday effect keeps service prices elevated

Hong Kong’s March headline CPI inflation came in broadly inline with expectations, rising 3.0%oya (J.P. Morgan: 3.1%; consensus: 2.9%). On a seasonally adjusted basis, the index rose 0.2%m/m sa in March, compared to 0.7% in February. Netting out the effects of the one-off relief measures, CPI inflation rose 0. l%m/m sa, or up 2.9%oya in March (vs. 3. l%oya in February).

Timing of Easter holiday keeps service price inflation elevated in March. Following the Lunar New Year holidays in February, Easter holiday this year fell at the end of March, compared to the beginning of April last year. Service prices rose 1.7%m/m sa in March, following a gain of 0.8% in February, in part driven by higher package tour prices. Meanwhile food prices, which jumped in February due to cold weather and Lunar New Year effects, eased 0.4%m/m sa in March (vs. +1.7% in February), but still remained at relatively elevated levels. Prices in most other categories remained benign, with clothing, durable goods, transportation, and alcohol/tobacco prices all easing in March.

Cloudy growth outlook to keep inflation in check. The government continued to highlight that the cloudy economic growth outlook should keep price pressure tame in the near term. Indeed, the employment report released this week showed that prolonged weakness in retail and tourism related sectors have fed through to the labor market, with the unemployment rate ticking up to 3.4%, a level not seen since 2013 (Figure 4). Unemployment not only rose in the retail and tourism sectors, but also major industries such as trade, manufacturing and construction. We expect labor market conditions to deteriorate further this year, with slowing wage growth which will likely keep price pressure benign.
