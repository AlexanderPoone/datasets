  • •    What about the question of term premia? Our long-term fair value framework uses a measure of inflation vol as a proxy for macroeconomic uncertainty, based on the exponentially weighted 5y moving volatility on y/y CPI that places more weight on recent observations. This proxy is shown in Figure 3, which suggests that this uncertainty peaked at close to 3% in Sep22 and has declined to just under 2.5% currently. Essentially, the fact that yields have now converged with levels implied by the long-term fail-value framework suggests that markets have priced in fully this rise in macroeconomic uncertainty, after having been consistent with an implied inflation vol level of 1.8% in early August and 1.6% in early June. This compares to a long-run average of the inflation vol measure of around 1.3%. In other words, the model implies that, after discounting a significant proportion of the rise in macro-economic uncertainty over the past two-and-a-half years, markets now appear to have priced in an extended period of elevated macro uncertainty. That said, this is not the only variable in the model that relates to term premia, as one of the explicit aims of QE has been to suppress term premia.




  • •    Our long-term fair value framework suggests a fair value for lOy real yields of around 2.25% currently, or around 4.65% after adding back lOy ahead inflation expectations from the Philly Fed survey. The continuation of the selloffin bonds that started in May but accelerated in September has seen lOy nominal yields briefly trade above 4.8% before retracing to around 4.75% at the time of writing, with lOy real yields also just above 2.4%. The longterm fair value framework has implied a higher level of yields than market levels for most of the period since late 2018, meaning that the fair value gap appears to have closed for the first time in five years.
