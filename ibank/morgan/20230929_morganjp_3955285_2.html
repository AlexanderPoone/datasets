Adding to the technical pressures, the relentless selloff and long-end steepening in recent weeks has made delivery options in bond futures valuable and interesting, and the prospect of shifts in the cheapest-to-deliverbond has drawn considerable interestfrom marketparticipants this week. Treasury futures contracts are physically settled, with shorts ultimately required to make delivery of one oftlieeligible bonds in thedelivery month. For the Decemberbond futures contract (the "USZ3" contract), bonds ranging from the 3.5% Feb ’39 to the 3% Feb ’49 are eligible for delivery; however, one of these bonds is usually cheapest to deliver, and this CTD bond tends to drive the duration characteristics and pricing of the contract. CTDs can change as yields and curve change - in particular, a sustained rise in yields in the 15- to 25-year sector of the curve, and/or a steepening of that part of the curve, can cause longer maturity bonds to eventually cheapen enough to become more optimal to deliver, causing the CTD to shift longer and potentially causing a further extension in the duration of theUSZ3 contract.
