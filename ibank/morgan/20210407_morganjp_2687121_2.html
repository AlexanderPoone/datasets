Introduction to NLP

Natural Language Processing, or NLP for short, is broadly defined as the automatic manipulation of natural language, like speech and text, by software. The study of natural language processing has been around for more than 50 years and grew out of the field of linguistics with the rise of computers.

NLP the Complicated Old Way

One of our earliest Machine Learning papers looked at “Extracting Sentiment from News: Machine Learning in Big Data for the Classification of News Sentiment for Equities” (9/Oct/2017). The paper used NLP and some more traditional Machine Learning (ML) techniques to forecast sentiment based on analyst report text, with reasonable accuracy in the order of 48%-72%.

However, the drawback of these models is the requirement for a long pipeline with many stages requiring hyper-parameter or modelling meta-decisions:

  • 1.    ingest raw data

  • 2.    removing case

  • 3.    [negating negatives] “declining profit” > [neg_profit] better than [decline, profit])

  • 4.    word tokenizing

  • 5.    [Part of Speak tagging]

  • 6.    removing stop-words

  • 7.    term frequency counting

  • 8.    [normalization]

  • 9.    unigram and bigram identification

  • 10.    Chi2 filtering

  • 11.    vectorization (Word2Vec)

  • 12.    [re] normalization

  • 13.    modelling

Building a custom NLP pipeline to predict sentiment of headline text is the focus of this paper. It is intended for those interested in building their own system using State of the Art NLP model as the base. If you would prefer to utilize off the shelf systems, please see our other reports covering; Accrete, Alexandria. ChinaScope, RavenPack, and other major vendors offering sentiment such as; FactSet, Refinitiv & Bloomberg.
