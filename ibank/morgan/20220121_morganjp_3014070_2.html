QT in focus as Fed meets, BoC hikes

Even as growth momentum moderates, the march toward central bank policy normalization continues. At next week’s meeting we expect the Fed to signal that conditions for liftoff will soon be met, and lay the table for a 25bp hike in March. While we see a low chance that the Fed will speed up the taper at this meeting, the press conference and the FOMC minutes (released three weeks later) should further outline the parameters of the Fed’s runoff plans, which we expect to be phased in from July.

The Bank of Canada also meets next week, when we expect the first of five 25bp hikes this year. Runoff of the BoC’s asset holdings is likely during 2H22. Tightening labor markets have accelerated exit plans for other DM central banks as

well. We expect faster Euro area wage growth this year to warrant an end to ECB asset purchases in December with liftoff early next year; hawkish ECB minutes this week support our call. Robust labor market and inflation data this week add pressure on the BoE to hike again in Februaiy, which also would trigger the start of balance sheet runoff. A strong jobs report leaves us now expecting the RBA to bring an early end to its QE program at next month’s meeting.

Balance sheet reduction by DM central banks will begin this year and accelerate into next, with asset runoff among most English-speaking central banks and the wind down of pandemic-related lending schemes by the ECB and BoJ leading to a $1.6tn net reduction in holdings by end-2023—about 15% of the post-pandemic buildup. Central banks’ expressed preferences are to use policy rates as their primary discretionary tool this cycle and put the balance sheet on “autopilot.” But the impact of QT on financial conditions, and therefore on the likely path for DM policy rates, is complex. Markets are pricing shallow and abbreviated hiking cycles against our forecast for elevated inflation and above-trend growth through the end of next year, and appear to view QT as a substitute for rate hikes. This implies an attenuated effect on financial conditions, and other research suggests a modest 20 to 50bp boost to longer-term bond yields from the Fed QT we project through end-2023. As such, there is a risk that DM central banks will need to tighten more than current market pricing to bring inflation sustainably back to target.

China rebounds but questions remain

The projected stabilization in China's growth this year is an important part of the global outlook. As reported this week, China’s real GDP grew 4%oya last quarter—a step down from the 4.9%oya outturn in 3Q21 but still a much-stronger-than-expected 7.5% annualized gain on a quarterly sequential basis. Factory output rebounded last quarter but still posted only a 3.1%ar gain, suggesting strong support from services as pandemic drags faded. Momentum in manufacturing output is quite strong into year-end, likely reflecting inventory restocking and stronger foreign sales. According to this week’s December activity data, final domestic demand growth remains soft on balance as retail sales and infrastructure spending continue to disappoint. Manufacturing investment remained the bright spot at year-end, supporting the broader signs of a rebound in global capex in December.
