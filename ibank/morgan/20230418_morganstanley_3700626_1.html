Ten IMF Takeaways

The IMF meetings turned out to be more positive than in October, arguably a Low bar, but also confirmed that the IMF will only keep growing in importance to EM. In sovereign credit, our preference for IG over HY remains. In Local markets, we do see value in rates but would fade the optimism in EM FX.

  • 1)    Weak growth, yet could well get weaker Despite an already weak growth outlook by the IMF, we see risks to the downside for its near-term growth forecasts, with Asia the key exception, where we are more bullish.

  • 2)    Rates to stay high for now: We are in agreement with the IMF that rates need to remain high over the next 12-18 months but also that neutral rates should eventually return to pre-pandemic levels.

  • 3)    Fiscal consolidation not as easy as made out: The IMF focus is rightly on fiscal consolidation, yet realistic options for doing so are much harder to come by, given that we are far from ‘boom times'. Also, fiscal multipliers deserve more attention.

  • 4)    More IMF programmes coming: A third of EM Bl countries are now in active IMF programmes, with more likely, in particular within SSA. Ivory Coast, Senegal and Ethiopia are all on the horizon, with Kenya also looking to upsize its programme. Ways to help Ecuador and Argentina were also discussed. The RSF is seen as a key mechanism to get additional funding to EM and demand is high, but actual disbursement is slow, in our view.

  • 5)    IMF interest expensesa growing problem: Most countries are now paying 4-6% on outstanding balances. While any change to policies looks unlikely in the near term, it means that an increasing number of IMF programmes only serve to repay existing money owed to the IMF.

  • 6)    Alternative financing sources beyond the IMF are key: Multilaterals need to step up more, potentially made possible by new SDR bonds. GCC financing has been more important for many countries already but this is also expected to come with increasing conditionalities, which may actually return some leverage to the IMF.

  • 7)    Overall, sentiment on EM was more upbeat than in October but problems remain, especially in sovereign credit.

  • 8)    In sovereign credit, we share many participants' view on continued high dispersion, with our preference being for IG over HY: For implementing a more optimistic outlook for EM but without taking as high credit risks, we suggest adding Mexico, Panama, Saudi Arabia, Oman and South Africa.
