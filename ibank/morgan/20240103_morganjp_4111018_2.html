Asia Pacific Economic Research Ten questions about China in 2024 03 January 2024


Executive summary

Although post-reopening recovery remained the major theme for China in 2023, the path has been bumpy and uneven.

Activity rebounded strongly in 1Q (10.4%q/q saar) after the rapid ending of COVID-related drags, but then slumped in 2Q (-0.8% q q saar). Encouragingly, economic activity has been bottoming since August, with policy shifting to become growth-friendly in 2H. Meanwhile, performance has been divergent across sectors. The housing market witnessed the worst downturn in China’s history. By contrast, beneficiary sectors in China’s structural transformation, e.g. new energy and NEV (new energy vehicles) sectors, made remarkable progress, and China is on track to becoming the largest auto exporting country.

As we turn towards 2024, we think the government will likely set the annual growth target unchanged at “around 5%”. While the government is unlikely to introduce a massive policy stimulus (Question 1), the growth-friendly policy stance will continue into 2024, with strengthened coordination between fiscal and monetary policies (Question 2). As the impact of modest easing measures will be reflected in the coming quarters, we expect the economy can maintain its recovery momentum in 1Q24 (5.1%q/q saar), before moderating to trend growth in 2H24 (4.3%q/q saar). Our forecast of 2024 growth stands at 4.9%.

An important task in 2024 is to manage the downside risk in the economy, particularly from the housing market correction and its spillover risks. Housing faces problems on both demand and supply sides, which will persist in 2024 (Question 3). The government’s housing stabilization efforts may not be sufficient to stabilize the housing market in the near term, yet public housing can partly offset the housing drag. In the absence of a housing market recoveiy, policy makers will step up efforts to mitigate risks arising from local government hidden debt. There may also be increasing risks associated with the banking sector, especially for regional banks, e.g. credit quality concerns, NIM compression and capital adequacy pressure (Question 4).

China also faces a problem of demand-supply imbalance and consequent deflation pressure. Even though consumption was the primary growth driver in 2023 (contributing about 80% to GDP growth in 2023), the consumption recoveiy has been lagging behind that in production. Income growth and adjustment in household savings jointly contribute to consumption recovery, but the absence of policy support for consumption has been a caveat (Question 5). Deflation pressure will likely fade in 2024, with the turnaround in global commodity prices and domestic pork prices, but low inflation will stay along with insufficient domestic demand (Question 6).

The shift in policy focus to “stability” does not mean the government will abandon other policy objectives. In fact, structural transformation has contributed to divergent performance across sectors. The government prioritizes technological innovation and modernizing the industrial system (Question 7). New economy sectors have grown rapidly, but not sufficiently to offset the decline in headwind sectors (most notably the housing market). The CEWC called for a balance between the two, i.e. maintaining the lift from new sectors but reducing the drag from the downturn sectors.

While China’s economic performance is mainly domestic driven, external conditions matter. Exports (in USD terms) declined in 2023, but in volume terms maintained positive growth. We expect export performance to be stable in 2024, with mitigated pressure on portfolio outflows and CNY exchange rate (Question 8). Nonetheless, global supply chain relocation seems to have accelerated since the Ukraine war, as resilience-based considerations seem to prevail efficiency-based considerations amid rising geopolitical tension, in addition to the change in comparative advantages and the US-Chi-na relationship. In response, China has made efforts to diversify exports, to invest overseas and promote self-sufficiency in technologies (Question 9).

2024 also brings a record number of elections across the world, including Taiwan’s election in January and a US presidential election in November. While elections often have little impact on underlying macro trends, markets have typically been volatile when facing geopolitical uncertainties. US-Chi-na strategic competition will persist. The competition in technology, financial and human rights areas may become more complicated into the election year (Question 10).
