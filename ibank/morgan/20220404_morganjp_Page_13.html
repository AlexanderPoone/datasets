IQ. The rapid rise in consumer imports points to strong domestic demand even through COVID-19-related setbacks in recent months (Figure 2). Capital goods imports also maintained their positive momentum, tracking a more than 5% annualized gain in IQ following six consecutive quarters of average gains of over 25%ar.

Marking up 1Q GDP, for a change

In addition to strong February trade data, the March business surveys were almost universally strong. In the IMEF March PMIs output of both manufacturing and sendees increased in the month, particularly sendees output, which bounced to 54.8 from 52.5. New orders and employment also increased across the board, while manufacturing inventories dropped. Other business surveys sent a similarly upbeat signal about growth momentum late last quarter. All-industry business confidence remained high, manufacturing production was strong—despite an expected cooling in export demand—and in services perceived income/demand indicators surged in the month. On these latter, perceived income in the services sector jumped to 56.4 in March from 50.1, with broad-based gains across sub-industries. Two things are worth noting: underlying demand does indeed look strong through COVID-19 related setbacks, and business-related services are starting to recover more notably after the massive drop on the back of changes to labor regulation in 3Q21. With this in mind, we markedly raised our IQ GDP growth forecast from 3.5%ar to 6%, and, despite trimming 2Q-4Q GDP, we raise our full-year growth forecast to 1.9%y/y from 1.5%.

Banxico minutes to err of the hawkish side

Next week, Banxico will release the minutes to the March meeting when the board unanimously decided to hike 50bp to 6.5% as widely expected. The bank decided to shift gears in November, acknowledging lingering inflation concerns and the start of a tighter bias by major central banks, particularly the Fed. We believe the start of the Fed hiking cycle last month was the main reason the board agreed unanimously to hike 50bp, and the minutes will shed some light on whether Banxico can sustain that now that our US economists see the Fed also shifting gears to 50bp in May and in June.

With the Bank updating its inflation path modestly to still well below our forecast, we believe the staff is being too complacent on the implications of the ongoing conflict in Europe and is relying too much on energy subsidies and the strength of the currency. We acknowledge our call for inflation to end at 7.2% this year is well above Banxico’s (5.5% for 4Q), but the ongoing generalized and sustained increments in core prices suggest there are significant risks to the upside for Banxico.

We believe the board discussed a 75bp hike, but such an aggressive move probably had little support on the board. The discussion on economic activity probably centered on the fact that Mexico has not regained its pre-pandemic output yet and that this won’t happen until early 2023 at best.
