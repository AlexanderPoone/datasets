EM debt-dedicated flows have improved materially over the past four weeks, with US$3.6 billion in outflows being registered. This contrasts to the US$9.2 billion in outflows registered over the prior four weeks. The flows improvement was visible both in hard currency, where outflows fell by US$3.2 billion, to US$1.7 billion over the past four weeks, and in local currency, where outflows fell by US$2.4 billion, to US$1.7 billion over the past four weeks.

On the hard currency side, the flows improvement over the past four weeks was mainly driven by sovereign funds, which saw US$542 million in inflows. Mixed sovereign/corporate and corporate-only funds continued to see outflows, at US$1.4 billion and US$893 million, respectively. Flows were decently balanced between global-mandated and regional/country funds but there was once again clear divergence between ETFs, which registered US$987 million in inflows, and non-ETFs, which saw US$2.7 billion in outflows.

On the local currency side, outflows were fairly balanced between global-mandated and regional/country funds. Much of the outflow of regional/country funds materialised out of China local funds, which saw US$702 million in outflows versus a US$863 million net outflow for all regional/country funds. Outflows were skewed to non-ETFs, which saw US$1.2 billion in outflows, but ETFs also saw outflows of US$447 million.

The previous four weeks have been characterised by volatile week-on-week flows, with EM fluctuating between near-flat flows and moderate outflows as the core rate rally gathered pace. The volatility was particularly noticeable on the hard currency side. The relationship between rates and flows to EM credit isn't always straightforward. Notably, while a fall in rates generally would draw money into the asset class, that clearly isn't the case when the fall is driven by risk-off dynamics, as was the case in March 2023.
